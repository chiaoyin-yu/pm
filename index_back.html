Consumer Behavior Research for Cooking Appliances
— Reducing Decision Anxiety from Choice Overload

The client’s core problem was that the product line had expanded, but conversion did not increase accordingly. Users showed clear decision anxiety during selection, but the pain point was unclear. I designed a three-stage research process to identify the root causes and provide concrete strategic directions.

Role: Problem Definition → Research Design → Stakeholder Briefing
  • Aligned with the business team on the core question: “Why are more features not leading to higher conversion?”
  • Designed a three-stage research process — segmented recruitment, pre-interview homework, and in-depth interviews — to capture real behavior rather than on-the-spot rationalization.
  • Segmented recruitment by age, children, cooking frequency, and purchase intent to capture differences across user groups.
  • Translated interview insights into strategic recommendations: each segment needed different information structures and trust-building approaches.
Key Findings → Strategic Recommendations
  • Too many options were the root problem; users needed a clear comparison framework, not longer feature lists.
  • Trust-building came before feature quantity — reputation, brand familiarity, and information consistency were key conversion factors.
  • Users with children or high cooking frequency and users who were single or cooked less often followed completely different decision logic, making a one-size-fits-all communication strategy ineffective.

What is Vibe Coding? Using AI to converge thinking and build a mental prototype before a single line is written. Product logic first, AI execution second.

Vibe Coding — Building Products
from Real Problems

Vibe coding is not about writing code — it's about using AI as a build partner while keeping product thinking in the driver's seat. Every project here started from a personal pain point: define the problem, design the flow, prompt with intention, and steer the output.

1 · Problem Definition

"What should we eat today?" is a question most people face multiple times a day — and it drains far more mental energy than it should. The real problem is not a lack of options, but an overload of them with no clear decision path.

  • User pain point: open-ended meal choices create daily decision fatigue, especially when preferences and restrictions vary between people
  • Root cause: existing solutions (delivery apps, food maps) expand choices — they solve discovery, not decision. The decision burden stays entirely on the user
  • Insight: the goal is not to find the perfect meal — it's to stop thinking about it. Users want the decision made for them, not more options to evaluate
2 · Solution Design

If the core problem is decision burden, the solution must take that burden away entirely — not help users decide better. This led to one core design principle: the app decides, the user just accepts or re-rolls.

  • Chose randomness as the core UX mechanic — not filtering or sorting, but a single-tap suggestion that removes choice from the user entirely
  • Designed preference settings as lightweight guardrails (cuisine type, dietary restrictions), not rigid rules — users can still get a random result within a loose boundary
  • Rejected a search or filter model: those interfaces assume users know what they want — the opposite of the target user state
  • Designed the full UX flow before any prompt: Onboarding → Preference Setup → Random Suggestion → Accept or Re-roll — keeping interaction to a single tap at the core moment
4 · North Star Metric
North Star Metric
% of sessions where the user accepts the first or second suggestion (without re-rolling more than twice)
Why: if users are re-rolling many times, the randomness mechanic is failing — it has become another form of browsing. The North Star captures whether the product is actually removing decision burden, not just delaying it.
  • Supporting metric 1: Daily active return rate — does the product become a daily habit, or a novelty used once?
  • Supporting metric 2: Preference edit frequency — if users constantly change preferences before getting a result, the guardrail design may be too rigid
  • Metric I intentionally excluded: time-to-decision. Faster is not always better — a user who pauses to think before accepting may actually be more satisfied with the result

World Coffee Master Guide — Turning a Travel Problem into a Product

1 · Problem Definition

You're at the gym, ready to train. The machine you planned to use is taken. Most people wait, skip, or give up — because without knowing what else trains the same muscle, there's no path forward.

  • User pain point: when a machine is taken, users don't know what to substitute — so they wait or abandon the set entirely
  • Compounding problem: switching to an unfamiliar machine means searching mid-session for how to use it — breaking focus and wasting rest time
  • Long-term pattern: people end up using only the machines they already know — no progression, no variety
  • Insight: the goal isn't to learn more exercises — it's to keep this session going. The knowledge is a byproduct.
2 · Target User

Someone who has been going to the gym for a while — not a beginner, but not yet fluent in the full equipment floor. They know what muscle group they want to train today, but they've been defaulting to the same handful of machines they already know. When one of those machines is taken, they're stuck — because they've never had a reason to explore the others.

  • Not a beginner: beginners need structured guidance, not substitutes
  • Not an advanced lifter: they already know how to adapt on the fly
  • The product lives in the middle — someone with habits and goals, but knowledge gaps that still get in the way
3 · Solution Design

One constraint drove every design decision: the answer has to be actionable within 60 seconds — the length of a typical rest period.

  • Rejected a full workout planner: generating a plan requires too many inputs and decisions — the opposite of what a mid-session user needs
  • Limited results to 2–3 alternatives: not the most options, but the fastest path to action. More choices = more burden.
  • AI earns its place here: real-time + knowledge-dense + personalized by muscle group — three conditions that make static databases too rigid and manual search too slow
4 · North Star Metric
North Star Metric
% of sessions where the user continues the planned muscle group after equipment is taken
Why: if users get an alternative but don't act on it, the product failed. The North Star isn't 'suggestions given' — it's 'training continued'. In MVP stage, this is approximated by alternative adoption rate — whether users click through to a demo video after receiving a suggestion.
  • Metric intentionally excluded: session duration. Shorter is fine — if you got what you needed fast, that's the product working.

6 · What Vibe Coding Taught Me

Building with AI is fast — fast enough to make you realize speed was never the problem.

I had a working prototype in two hours. But finishing it made me ask: who opens this every day? What moment in their life does this live in? Those questions, vibe coding can't answer.
